The system is corporate

Of 15,039 eviction cases filed in 2024, 88% were filed by corporate plaintiffs — LLCs, LPs, and property management companies. The remaining 12% were filed by individuals.

This is not a system of individual landlords resolving individual disputes. It is a bulk-processing operation run by institutional actors.

Two streams of outcomes

Cases split into three outcomes. More than half end in an eviction granted to the landlord. Nearly a third are dismissed or settled — the filing served its purpose as leverage without requiring a judgment. The rest close administratively.

This divergence is the core of the dual infrastructure: the same court produces both bargaining resolutions and displacement judgments.

The representation asymmetry

In 91.1% of cases, the landlord has an attorney and the tenant does not. In only 1.4% of cases do both sides have counsel. Fifteen cases — out of fifteen thousand — had a defendant attorney but no plaintiff attorney.

The displacement pipeline

Of the 7,920 cases where eviction was granted, 7,150 had a writ of possession posted — meaning the sheriff was sent to physically remove the tenant. That's a 90.4% enforcement rate.

From filing to posted writ, the median case takes 50 days. In under two months, a paper filing becomes a person removed from their home.
